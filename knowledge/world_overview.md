# Toronto 2047 — World Overview

## The City

Toronto in 2047 is a city of brutal contrasts. Glass spires owned by megacorporations pierce a sky perpetually stained amber by atmospheric processors. Below them, seventeen million people live in varying degrees of corporate dependency — or desperate resistance to it.

The physical city sprawls from the rejuvenated Lakeshore Enclave in the south to the industrial Fringe districts in the north. Underground transit networks called **Deeplines** connect the districts, though most lines pass through corporate checkpoints. Surface streets are patrolled by contracted security forces, not city police — the Toronto Metropolitan Police were dissolved in 2038 after the **Privatization Accords**.

## How It Got Here

**2028 — The Collapse Cascade**
A cascading failure in global financial infrastructure triggered hyperinflation across North America. National governments proved unable to respond. Megacorporations, sitting on massive private reserves, stepped in to provide essential services: power, water, food distribution, medical care.

**2031 — The Corporate Sovereignty Act**
The Canadian federal government, desperate for stabilization funding, passed the Corporate Sovereignty Act. It granted five registered megacorporations limited territorial rights within designated urban zones. Each corp was permitted to operate private security forces, adjudicate internal disputes under corporate law, and collect "service fees" from residents within their zones.

Toronto was designated a **Shared Sovereignty City** — meaning multiple corporations hold overlapping territorial rights, which is the source of constant conflict.

**2035 — The Augmentation Boom**
Helix Dynamics commercialized affordable cybernetic augmentation. Within five years, 40% of the Toronto population had at least one implant. The city became a testing ground. Augmented citizens were offered credit packages by Helix in exchange for biometric data — a deal millions accepted without reading the fine print.

**2039 — The Blackout War**
A six-week period of open corporate warfare between Axiom Systems and OmniCore Energy over control of the city's power grid. Three districts were plunged into darkness. Hundreds died. The conflict ended with the **Lakeshore Accords**, a negotiated territorial division still in effect today. The ruins of the contested sectors — now called the **Char Zones** — are ungoverned and lawless.

**2044 — The Phantom Breach**
An anonymous hacker collective penetrated Vantage Data's central repository and leaked dossiers on 11 million Toronto residents. The files revealed that Vantage had been profiling citizens for corporate clients, flagging dissidents, and selling predictive behavior scores. Public fury was immense but short-lived — three of the five megacorps controlled major media channels.

**2047 — Present Day**
The city operates in a tense equilibrium. Corporate security forces enforce order within their zones. The Underbelly districts are essentially ungoverned. Freelance operators — called **ghosts** in street slang — work the cracks between corporate power: stealing data, running interference, extracting people, moving contraband. It's dangerous, lucrative, and the only real freedom left.

## Atmosphere and Tone

- **Permanent night-feel**: Smog, light pollution, and towering corp towers mean natural sunlight rarely reaches street level in the inner districts
- **Surveillance everywhere**: Vantage Data runs the city's camera network; every major intersection has facial recognition
- **Augmentation as class marker**: Premium augments signal wealth; grey-market implants signal desperation
- **Language**: Street slang mixes English, French Canadian, Tamil, Cantonese, and Tagalog — Toronto's demographic reality amplified
- **Currency**: Credits (corp-issued digital currency) dominate formal commerce; physical **shards** (encrypted data chips with stored value) are used in the underground economy

## The Power Balance

No single corporation controls Toronto entirely. The five megacorps — Nexus Corp, Axiom Systems, Helix Dynamics, Vantage Data, and OmniCore Energy — operate in a balance of mutual deterrence, economic interdependence, and occasional covert warfare. Each has different territorial holdings, different security capabilities, and different vulnerabilities.

Between them, factions like the Collective, the Silk Network, and the Iron Veil mercenary guild operate in the grey zones, serving whoever pays or pursuing their own agendas.

Freelance crews — like the player's team — exist in the most dangerous position of all: no corporate backing, no faction protection, everything to gain, everything to lose.

## The Rules of the Street

1. **No wet work on civilians** — killing non-combatants draws unwanted attention from every faction
2. **Pay your debts** — the underground economy runs on reputation
3. **Don't burn a fixer who hasn't burned you** — you'll need them again
4. **Vantage is always watching** — assume any camera in the city feeds to Vantage Data
5. **The corps don't forgive** — if a megacorp IDs your crew, you have 48 hours before Razors arrive
